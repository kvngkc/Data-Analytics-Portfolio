During the first quarter of 2026, Cyclistic users took a total of 1,072,630 clean, verified rides. 1. Business Objective & Strategic Directive

Cyclistic has established a robust market presence in Chicago, deploying a high-quality fleet of over 5,800 geo-tracked bikes across hundreds of physical docking stations. Historically, the marketing strategy focused broadly on user acquisition across two main tiers: casual riders (purchasing single-ride or full-day passes) and annual members (paying a recurring annual fee).

Financial audits indicate that annual members generate substantially higher, more predictable margins and secure the long-term cash flow required to scale fleet maintenance and infrastructure. Lily Moreno, Director of Marketing, has issued a clear strategic objective: maximize the conversion of existing casual riders into annual members.

"The goal is not simply to acquire new users, but to capture the extensive, high-value usage occurring on our network today and guide those casual riders into long-term member commitments."

2. Data Engineering & Quality Assurance

To ensure high analytical rigor and data integrity, we constructed a data engineering pipeline in Python (pandas) to ingest, clean, and validate monthly Divvy-trip datasets spanning Q1 2026. A detailed data-cleaning protocol was established to strip out noise, system tests, and erroneous logs.

The Cleansing Protocol

  • Deduplication: 100% verification of unique ride_id fields. No duplicates were found.
  • Test Data Removal: Filtered out any trips starting or ending at internal test, temporary, or maintenance stations (specifically matching "test", "temp", "maintenance", "hq", or "base"). This removed 2,341 rides.
  • Duration-Based Filtering: Trips with durations under 1 minute (often accidental starts) or exceeding 24 hours (usually indicating unreturned/stolen bikes or docking errors) were dropped. This removed 29,481 rides.
  • Dockless Imputation: To preserve valuable electric bike data that relies on on-street locking systems, missing station names and IDs were standardized to "On-Street (Dockless)" and "DOCKLESS".
  • Missing Coordinate Elimination: Removed 74 records missing essential coordinate metrics (latitude/longitude), guaranteeing compatibility for spatial mapping.

3. Behavioral Deep Dive: Members vs. Casual Users

Exploratory analysis on the cleaned Q1 2026 dataset reveals deep divergence in riding behaviors between the two customer segments. Understanding these unique behaviors is key to constructing a successful conversion model.

Annual Members: Commuters

Members are highly utility-oriented and demonstrate uniform, consistent habits. Their ride count peaks dramatically on weekdays, averaging 135k rides daily from Tuesday through Thursday, while dropping to 76,327 on Sundays.

Furthermore, members maintain a highly stable, short average trip length of 11.33 minutes. This stability indicates commute-to-work or utility-based trips, where the bike is treated as a core transit mechanism with minimal detour.

Casual Riders: Weekend Warriors

Casual riders exhibit classic leisure-based usage. Their numbers climb as the week progresses, peaking sharply on Saturdays (47,900) and remaining high on Sundays (40,902). They represent a massive 35.5% share of total weekend trips compared to just 20.2% on weekdays.

This group also rides much longer, with an average trip of 16.04 minutes (41.6% longer than members). This average escalates to 18.20 minutes on Saturdays and 19.00 minutes on Sundays, indicative of scenic, relaxed rides along the lakefront, parks, and retail districts.

5. Marketing Playbook & Conversion Initiatives

To convert these weekend-leisure riders into dedicated members, we propose three distinct marketing interventions rooted in our Q1 2026 findings.

1. The "Weekend Warrior" Targeted Pass Tier

Our data indicates that casual usage spikes 35% higher on Saturdays and Sundays than weekdays, and their trips are almost double the duration of commuter members. Traditional annual memberships focus on daily commute utility, which doesn't appeal to leisure riders.

Strategic Action: Introduce a "Weekend Leisure Pass" at a lower price point, providing unlimited weekend access. This pass serves as an entry-level trial, lowering the barrier to entry and creating a segment that can eventually be upsold to a standard full annual plan.

2. Localized Geo-Marketing Campaigns near Leisure Hotspots

Casual riders primarily originate and end trips in coastal and park environments (e.g., Millennium Park, Lake Shore Drive). Deploying physical and digital marketing in high-volume areas during peak leisure times will capture the user in their active riding state.

Strategic Action: Use geofenced mobile push notifications and localized physical branding at highly active weekend docking hubs. Real-time offers such as "Unlock free minutes today by starting a trial membership" can instantly capture spontaneous weekend riders.

3. Personalized "Savings Summaries" email workflows

Because casual riders keep bikes out much longer than members, they accumulate higher single-trip charges. Demonstrating how much they would have saved as members is a powerful tool to drive financial conversions.

Strategic Action: Create automated trigger emails for non-members after a long weekend ride (e.g., >15 mins). Deliver a personalized cost-saving summary: "You spent $12 this weekend. A member would have paid $0 for this ride. Click here to see how an annual membership pays for itself in just 8 trips."
